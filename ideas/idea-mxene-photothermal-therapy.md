## Idea

MXenes absorb near-infrared light and convert it to heat with high efficiency, so an injected or locally delivered MXene agent can be irradiated to thermally ablate a tumour. The more compelling version pairs mild photothermal heating with immune activation (STING agonism, checkpoint priming) so local ablation triggers a systemic anti-tumour response, and the tunable MXene surface carries the drug or agonist.

## Why now

Through 2025 MXene photothermal agents reached high conversion efficiency and, more importantly, moved past pure heating: papers show MXene mild-PTT synergising with STING activation for post-cryoablation cancer vaccines and MXene-doxorubicin hydrogels for combined chemo-photothermal therapy. The field is shifting from ablation to immuno-oncology combinations, where the value is higher.

## 3-month MVP

Produce a defined, GMP-plausible MXene formulation, measure its NIR conversion efficiency, and demonstrate in-vitro tumour-cell ablation. Pair this with a literature-grounded biodistribution and clearance risk assessment and an indication-selection memo favouring endoscopically or superficially accessible tumours. The output de-risks formulation and target choice before animal spend.

## Validation plan

Reproduce tumour regression in a syngeneic mouse model with the chosen MXene formulation, then generate first biodistribution and clearance data, the gating result for any IND.

## Notes

This carries a long capital and regulatory horizon and prior nanoparticle-ablation companies (Nanospectra, AuroLase) have found the commercial path hard against surgery and RFA. The differentiated bet is the immune-activating combination, not heating alone. Weak EF fit; better as a nanomedicine spinout with pharma partnership.
