## Idea

Wearable sweat and ISF sensors fail on the biomarkers people actually want (cortisol, sex hormones, cytokines) because those sit below the sensor's detection limit. A MOF/MXene/hydrogel patch layer captures and concentrates the target before it reaches the sensor, raising a sub-threshold analyte into readable range. Sold as the enabling layer, it makes a class of previously invisible analytes measurable across many wearable products.

## Why now

2025 brought the two missing pieces together: superhydrophilic hydrogel-enhanced conductive MOF sweat sensors with anti-biofouling surfaces, and thermoresponsive hydrogels that absorb sweat then release it on demand into a microfluidic channel. A preconcentration module is the natural synthesis, and nobody sells it as a standalone component yet.

## 3-month MVP

Build a benchtop capture module and measure the enrichment fold-factor it achieves for one lighthouse analyte, cortisol, in artificial sweat. Then demonstrate a quantitative release step that feeds a commercial electrochemical sensor, benchmarked against a no-capture baseline. The result is a concrete "we made an unreadable analyte readable" data point to take to sensor partners.

## Validation plan

Show the MOF/MXene hydrogel layer concentrates a spiked low-abundance analyte by a measured fold-factor and releases it quantitatively into a downstream sensor, the single claim the whole business rests on.

## Notes

The appeal is that this is a picks-and-shovels component, positioned upstream of every wearable sensor rather than competing with them, which fits a partnership/licensing model and generates high-fidelity biomarker data as a byproduct. Cambridge materials-chemistry route is strong; EF plausible with a wearables cofounder.
