## Idea
Wearable biochemical sensors hit a floor because the interesting markers (cortisol, cytokines, hormones) are present at very low concentrations in sweat and interstitial fluid. This patch adds a capture step: MOF and MXene surfaces trap and concentrate the target molecule before the sensor reads it, and the concentration step is powered by triboelectric harvesting rather than a battery. The wedge is reaching markers that flat electrochemical patches cannot see.

## Why now
MOF and reduced-graphene-oxide wearable sensors demonstrated low-concentration sweat-marker detection in 2024, and the field now explicitly names preconcentration as the route below the detection floor, while triboelectric harvesting can plausibly supply the drive energy.

## 3-month MVP
Measure the enrichment factor on the bench. Spike a low-abundance analyte into artificial sweat, capture it on a MOF or MXene surface, and compare the effective sensor reading against direct detection. Separately estimate whether a triboelectric harvest can meet the capture-and-release energy budget.

## Validation plan
Demonstrate a meaningful, repeatable enrichment factor for one clinically relevant low-abundance analyte, with characterized fouling over realistic wear times.

## Notes
Three coupled hard problems (MOF/MXene stability, on-body capture timing, harvested-energy budget). High ceiling, deep-tech risk profile, better matched to a Cambridge lab spinout than a three-month EF sprint.
