## Mechanism

MXenes are made by chemically etching the aluminium or other A-element layer out of a layered MAX-phase ceramic, leaving stacks of atom-thin transition-metal carbide or nitride sheets, of which titanium carbide (Ti3C2Tx) is the workhorse. The result is unusual: a material that conducts almost like a metal, exposes a very large surface area of roughly 1500 square metres per gram, carries a hydrophilic and chemically tunable surface, and absorbs strongly in the near infrared. Those properties let it act as a highly sensitive electrode, a flexible conductive film, or a photothermal agent that heats when illuminated.

## What it unlocks

The bench results are the draw. MXene electrodes have detected sweat biomarkers down to nanomolar concentrations, well below what conventional carbon electrodes reach, and their flexibility suits skin-conformal wearables and smart textiles. The same photothermal absorption enables light-triggered tumour ablation and antibacterial coatings, and the conductive inks are used in bioprinting. XPANCEO, a Dubai startup that raised roughly 250 million dollars, is building smart contact lenses from atom-thin 2D materials with integrated glucose and intraocular-pressure biosensors, one of the most ambitious commercial applications of the class. Concrete near-term targets are ultra-sensitive wearable and point-of-care sensor electrodes and photothermal therapy.

## Translational gap

The material fights its own commercialisation. Ti3C2Tx oxidises: it degrades in air, faster in water, and faster still under UV, so films and dispersions lose the very conductivity that makes them useful, which is fatal for shelf life and long wear. Long-term biocompatibility in and on the body is not settled, and the standard synthesis uses hydrofluoric acid, a hazardous route that complicates safe, reproducible scale-up. Honest TRL is 3, meaning proof of concept validated in the lab; almost every device claim rests on fresh material measured over short windows. The unlock is stabilisation, whether through surface passivation, oxidation-resistant storage, or HF-free synthesis, plus biocompatibility and stability data over device-relevant timeframes in real biofluids.

## Notes

Judge MXene device claims by the timescale of the demonstration. Nanomolar sensitivity measured on a freshly etched sheet is not the same as a sensor that holds calibration for a week on skin, and the gap between those two is the entire business risk.
