## One-liner

Science Corporation builds biohybrid brain and eye interfaces, most notably the Science Eye and the acquired PRIMA retinal implant, to restore vision and read from the brain at high bandwidth.

## Problem

Degenerative retinal diseases such as retinitis pigmentosa and dry age-related macular degeneration destroy the photoreceptors that convert light into neural signals, leaving patients blind while much of the downstream visual pathway remains intact. No approved therapy restores meaningful functional vision once photoreceptors are lost.

## Workaround

Patients rely on low-vision aids, mobility training, and assistive software, and earlier bionic-eye efforts delivered only crude light perception before most were discontinued. These approaches do not restore usable form or text vision.

## Entry wedge

Science entered through vision restoration, unveiling the Science Eye in November 2022 and then acquiring the clinically validated PRIMA retinal implant assets from bankrupt French company Pixium Vision in 2024, giving it a near-term regulatory-stage product.

## Method

The Science Eye pairs an optogenetic gene therapy that makes surviving retinal ganglion cells light-responsive with an ultra-thin, high-density microLED display implanted over the retina. PRIMA uses a sub-millimetre photovoltaic chip implanted under the retina that works with camera-equipped glasses to stimulate the remaining retinal neurons. In parallel, Science develops a biohybrid brain interface seeded with lab-grown neurons stimulated by light to integrate with a patient's own neural tissue.

## Validated by

In the PRIMA clinical trial, 80 percent of participants with advanced macular degeneration demonstrated meaningful improvement in visual acuity and could read letters, numbers, and words. Science closed a $230 million Series C in March 2026 at a $1.5 billion post-money valuation, bringing cumulative funding to about $490 million.

## Competes with

Neuralink and other high-bandwidth brain-computer interface companies on the neural-interface side, and retinal-prosthesis and optogenetic-vision efforts such as GenSight Biologics, Bionic Sight, and Nano Precision Medical on the vision-restoration side.

## Complements

AAV gene-therapy manufacturers, ophthalmic surgical networks, camera and display hardware suppliers, and clinical sites running low-vision rehabilitation programs.

## Value chain position

**Downstream of:** gene-therapy vector manufacturing, microLED and photovoltaic chip fabrication, and neurosurgical and ophthalmic implantation infrastructure.

**Upstream of:** patients and treating clinicians who receive the restored visual function, and the rehabilitation and care-delivery services that support implant recipients.

## Gaps

Long-term durability and biocompatibility of chronic retinal and brain implants remain unproven at scale, the Science Eye itself has less public clinical data than the acquired PRIMA program, and regulatory approval for a combined gene-therapy plus device product is a multi-year path.

## Notes

Founded in 2021 by Max Hodak, a Neuralink co-founder and former president. The company runs two linked bets: near-term vision restoration through PRIMA and the Science Eye, and a longer-horizon biohybrid high-bandwidth brain interface using lab-grown neurons stimulated optically. The 2024 Pixium acquisition converted a research-stage vision effort into a regulatory-stage commercial pipeline.
