## One-liner

Beam Therapeutics is the base-editing pioneer, developing precision medicines that chemically rewrite a single DNA letter without cutting both strands, with clinical programs spanning ex-vivo sickle cell editing and in-vivo liver editing for genetic disease.

## Problem

Most inherited diseases are caused by a single-letter DNA mutation, yet first-generation CRISPR cuts both strands and cannot make a clean single-base correction, risking unwanted insertions, deletions, and chromosomal rearrangements. Patients with disorders like sickle cell disease or alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency have relied on lifelong management rather than a precise genetic fix.

## Workaround

Chronic disease management: transfusions and hydroxyurea for sickle cell, augmentation therapy and transplant for AATD-related lung and liver disease, and nuclease CRISPR or gene-addition approaches that knock genes out or add copies rather than correcting the exact mutation.

## Entry wedge

BEAM-101 (risto-cel) was the wedge: an ex-vivo base edit of a patient's own blood stem cells that switches on fetal hemoglobin, chosen because the biology and the cell-therapy route are well understood from prior sickle cell programs. Base editing's single-base precision differentiates it from cut-based CRISPR, and the company is extending from that ex-vivo beachhead into in-vivo liver editing (BEAM-302 for AATD).

## Method

A base editor fuses a Cas protein to a deaminase and is guided by a guide RNA to a target site, where it chemically converts one DNA base to another (for example C-to-T or A-to-G) without a double-strand break. For BEAM-101, a patient's CD34+ blood stem cells are edited ex vivo in the HBG1/2 promoters to block BCL11A repression and raise fetal hemoglobin, then reinfused. For BEAM-302, a lipid nanoparticle delivers the editor to the liver in vivo to correct the disease-causing AATD mutation directly. The ESCAPE program adds an antibody-based conditioning alternative to reduce the toxicity of stem-cell transplant preparation.

## Validated by

BEAM-101 received FDA Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy (RMAT) designation for sickle cell disease and reported supportive BEACON Phase 1/2 data at EHA 2025, with initial pivotal-relevant data expected in 2026. BEAM-302 delivered the first-ever clinical in-vivo genetic correction of a disease-causing mutation in AATD, establishing clinical proof of concept, with a 60 mg dose selected and a global pivotal cohort planned for the second half of 2026. Beam ended Q1 2026 with about 1.2 billion USD in cash and runway into mid-2029.

## Competes with

CRISPR Therapeutics and Vertex (Casgevy, approved ex-vivo sickle cell edit), bluebird bio (lovo-cel), and Intellia (in-vivo CRISPR) on sickle cell and liver editing; Verve/Eli Lilly on in-vivo liver base editing of lipid genes; and, for AATD, gene-addition and small-molecule programs. Prime Medicine competes on precise-correction editing more broadly.

## Complements

Base-editing IP licensors and David Liu's academic lab extending the toolkit, lipid-nanoparticle delivery suppliers, contract manufacturers of editors and cell products, authorized treatment centers and conditioning providers for ex-vivo therapy, and payers reimbursing one-time curative treatments.

## Value chain position

**Downstream of:** base-editing science from David Liu's lab, human genetics of fetal hemoglobin and AATD, and delivery and cell-manufacturing chemistry.

**Upstream of:** treating hematologists and hepatologists, authorized treatment centers, and payers reimbursing durable base-editing therapies, plus any future pharma partners on out-licensed assets.

## Gaps

No approved product; lead assets are Phase 1/2 with pivotal cohorts only beginning in the second half of 2026. The ex-vivo sickle cell route still requires genotoxic conditioning (which the ESCAPE program aims to reduce), and in-vivo editing beyond the liver is unproven. Durability of single-base edits over years and long-term off-target safety in patients remain open questions.
