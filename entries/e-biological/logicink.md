## One-liner

LogicInk makes tattoo-like skin patches that use programmable chemistry and biology, instead of electronics, to display real-time body and environmental signals as color changes.

## Problem

Most consumer health and environmental sensing requires a powered device, a battery, and a screen. That makes continuous, always-on monitoring of things like UV dose, hydration, or exposure inconvenient, expensive, and dependent on charging and connectivity.

## Workaround

Consumers rely on rough proxies: weather-app UV indices, sunscreen reapplication timers, or wearables and phone apps that estimate exposure indirectly rather than measuring it at the skin.

## Entry wedge

LogicInk UV (later the Suncare Signal), a temporary tattoo that changes color as it accumulates UV exposure and alerts the wearer when a safe daily minimal erythemal dose is reached. It is a low-cost, single-purpose consumer product that demonstrates the platform.

## Method

The core is a platform of temporary, tattoo-like biosensors branded "Signals" that encode logic into chemistry and biology rather than silicon. The sensing ink is formulated so that a target stimulus, ultraviolet light, a skin or sweat analyte, drives a chemical reaction that produces a visible, calibrated color change on the skin. For the UV product, the response is tuned against medical research on minimal erythemal dose so the readout maps to a meaningful exposure threshold. The architecture uses no electronics, no battery, and no reader, so the patch itself is both sensor and display. The company has publicly extended the concept toward cell-free and CRISPR-based instrument-free diagnostics, licensing Sherlock Biosciences CRISPR technology via the 221b Foundation to explore an instrument-free COVID-19 biosensor sticker, which is why the platform is classed here as cell-free synthetic biology applied to a wearable substrate.

## Validated by

First product LogicInk UV brought to market and covered by Fast Company, Wareable, and Engineering.com. NIH grant funding for an alcohol-consumption monitoring Signal. Partnership and CRISPR license agreement announced in 2021 with the 221b Foundation and Sherlock Biosciences. Website currently lists Suncare and Hydration Signals as available.

## Competes with

L'Oreal My UV Patch and other color-change UV dosimeter stickers, plus electronic consumer wearables that estimate UV and hydration indirectly.

## Complements

Sunscreen and skincare brands, dermatology and sun-safety education programs, and public-health screening efforts that want a cheap, reader-free readout at the skin.

## Value chain position

**Downstream of:** specialty chemistry and materials suppliers, synthetic-biology and cell-free reagent providers, and CRISPR IP licensors (Sherlock Biosciences via the 221b Foundation).

**Upstream of:** the consumer wearing the patch and any downstream skincare, wellness, or public-health workflow that acts on the visible readout.

## Gaps

Chemistry-only readouts are qualitative or semi-quantitative and cannot easily stream, log, or trend data the way electronic wearables do. Repeat-purchase economics for single-use cosmetic patches are unproven. The move from UV novelty toward regulated diagnostics (concussion, genetic predisposition, infectious disease) crosses into a much heavier regulatory and evidence burden than a cosmetic sun sensor.
