## One-liner

Provides insurance-covered virtual care for women in perimenopause and menopause, offering HRT prescribing, lifestyle support, and ongoing clinician relationships across all 50 states.

## Problem

Women in perimenopause and menopause face a care gap: OB-GYNs and primary care physicians have limited training in menopause management, hormone therapy is under-prescribed due to outdated guidelines, and symptoms are routinely dismissed or misattributed. The average woman waits years to receive a menopause diagnosis or appropriate HRT.

## Workaround

Direct-to-consumer HRT startups (Alloy, Evernow) without ongoing clinical relationship, gynecologists booking months out, primary care managing symptoms off-guideline, and over-the-counter supplements with limited efficacy.

## Entry wedge

Founded in 2021 by Joanna Strober and Sharon Meers. The founding insight was that menopause care is systematically underserved despite affecting 50M+ US women at any given time, and that telehealth could deliver specialist-equivalent care at scale. The entry wedge was insurance coverage: Midi accepted insurance from launch, positioning against cash-pay competitors and opening access to the mainstream patient population. Fortune 100 employer benefit partnerships accelerated B2B2C distribution.

## Method

Patients book a 30-minute virtual consultation with a Midi clinician trained specifically in perimenopause and menopause management. The clinician reviews full health history, current symptoms, and personal goals, then creates a personalized care plan that may include HRT (patches, pills, creams, vaginal rings), non-hormonal medications, supplements, and lifestyle coaching. Plans are adjusted iteratively through ongoing messaging and follow-up appointments. Midi is in-network with 80% of PPO plans, covering 45M+ women, and operates in all 50 states. The company has partnered with Fortune 100 employers to offer Midi as a workplace health benefit, and with health systems (Memorial Hermann) and benefits platforms (Progyny, Cleo).

## TAM logic

The global women's health market was valued at $100B+ and is growing rapidly as the femtech and menopause care segments attract institutional attention. In the US, 50M+ women are in perimenopause or menopause at any given time, representing a high-prevalence chronic condition with historically underfunded care. Midi's TAM includes both insurance reimbursement for clinical visits and the employer benefits spend on women's health. The $100B+ figure is a broad women's health market estimate; Midi's directly addressable market is narrower.

## Competes with

Alloy Health (HRT focused, DTC, cash-pay), Evernow (menopause, DTC, also pivoting to insurance), Gennev (menopause telehealth, acquired by Unified Women's Healthcare), Elektra Health (menopause education and coaching), Maven Clinic (women's health across fertility, maternity, menopause), and Hers (Hims & Hers women's health, GLP-1 and hormone prescribing).

## Complements

Employer benefits platforms (Progyny, Cleo, Maven), health systems for in-person escalation, lab providers for hormonal testing, pharmacies for HRT dispensing, and primary care platforms for comorbidity management.

## Value chain position

**Downstream of:** Insurance payer networks, employer benefits decision-makers, women's health advocacy and awareness channels
**Upstream of:** Primary care and specialist referral for comorbidities, pharmacy for HRT dispensing, mental health care for mood-related menopause symptoms

## Gaps

Midi's clinical focus is tightly defined (perimenopause and menopause); expansion into broader women's health (fertility, maternal health, general gynecology) would require significant clinical infrastructure investment. The HRT prescribing market has high clinical controversy risk: any new safety signal on HRT (reminiscent of the 2002 WHI study reversal) could suppress patient demand. Provider training quality at scale is unverified; clinical outcomes data from Midi-managed patients vs. standard care has not been published. The $1B valuation at Series D is steep relative to disclosed patient volume; revenue per patient will be modest given insurance reimbursement rates for telehealth visits.

## Notes

The Joanna Strober founder story is central to Midi's brand: she built the company after her own menopause experience highlighted the care gap. The Fortune 2026 profile describes Midi's ambition to become "the biggest consumer brand in women's health," signaling a long-term consumer brand play beyond clinical care. McKesson Ventures as a Series D investor is notable: McKesson is the largest pharmaceutical distributor in the US, which could facilitate pharmacy and medication access partnerships. The comparison set in femtech tends to cluster around fertility and maternal health; Midi's focus on midlife women is a less crowded niche with strong insurance tailwinds.
