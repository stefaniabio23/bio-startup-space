## One-liner

University of Cambridge spinout commercialising metal-organic frameworks as carriers for slow, controlled, and targeted drug delivery.

## Problem

Many drugs suffer from poor solubility, fast clearance, or off-target exposure, so delivering them at the right rate and place remains a formulation bottleneck.

## Workaround

Liposomes, polymer nanoparticles, and hydrogels, each with loading, stability, or release-control limitations that MOFs aim to improve on with high porosity and tunable chemistry.

## Entry wedge

Licensing MOF-based controlled-release formulation technology to pharmaceutical partners for specific payloads where existing carriers underperform on loading or release control.

## Method

Metal-organic frameworks, highly porous crystalline lattices of metal nodes and organic linkers, encapsulate drug payloads in their pores and release them slowly and controllably, with the framework chemistry tuned for targeting and biocompatibility. Technology developed by David Fairen-Jimenez and team at the Cambridge Department of Chemical Engineering and Biotechnology.

## Validated by

Underlying Cambridge research on MOF drug delivery; early expressed interest from pharmaceutical companies. Company-specific clinical or product validation is thin in public sources.

## Competes with

Coordination Pharmaceuticals and Nanobiotix (nMOF and nanoparticle therapeutics), Numat and NuMat / MOF materials companies, and conventional liposomal and polymer drug-delivery platforms.

## Complements

Pharmaceutical formulation and development teams, drug-delivery CDMOs, and drug developers with payloads limited by solubility or release kinetics.
