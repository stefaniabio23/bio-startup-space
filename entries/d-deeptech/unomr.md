## One-liner

ETH Zurich spin-out using a serial interface nanopore to read protein modifications, starting with glycosylation, at the single-molecule level.

## Problem

Glycosylation and other post-translational modifications drive disease (autoimmune conditions, breast and pancreatic cancer) but are hard to map site-specifically; mass spec needs large samples and misses single-molecule heterogeneity.

## Workaround

Mass-spectrometry glycoproteomics, lectin and antibody affinity assays, and enzymatic digestion, all bulk methods that average over molecules and demand substantial sample.

## Entry wedge

Site-specific glycosylation analysis from small sample volumes and low protein concentrations, targeting biomarker and therapeutic-discovery use cases where existing tools are blind to single-molecule modification detail.

## Method

Interface nanopore (iNP) technology threads proteins through a serial nanopore sensor; current signatures reveal modifications directly, requiring only basic sample purification, small volumes, and low concentrations. Core technology developed during the founders' PhDs at ETH Zurich.

## Validated by

Founded summer 2025 by Til Schlotter (CEO) and Julian Hengsteler (CTO) from ETH Zurich. Received CHF 150,000 from Venture Kick and named a Venture Leaders Biotech company. Grant funding from Gebert Ruf Stiftung.

## Competes with

Portal Biotech and Genopore (nanopore protein sequencing and identification), Quantum-Si, and mass-spec glycoproteomics workflows (Thermo, Bruker).

## Complements

Proteomics sample-prep vendors, pharma glycoprotein therapeutic developers, and food-science quality analysis; nanopore-pore engineering lineage shared with the broader sequencing ecosystem.

## Value chain position

**Downstream of:** nanopore sensor fabrication, protein sample prep
**Upstream of:** glycoproteomics analysis, biomarker discovery, biologic drug characterisation

## Gaps

Thin data. Very early (pre-seed, founded 2025); no commercial product, no throughput or accuracy benchmarks yet. Whether iNP scales beyond glycosylation to general PTM analysis is unproven.
