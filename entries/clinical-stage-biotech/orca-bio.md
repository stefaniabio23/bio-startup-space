## One-liner

Late-stage biotech developing precision-sorted allogeneic cell therapy (Orca-T) as a superior replacement for conventional bone marrow transplant in blood cancers, with a BLA under FDA Priority Review.

## Problem

Conventional allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) carries severe risks of graft-versus-host disease (GvHD), a life-threatening immune complication where donor immune cells attack the patient. GvHD drives high morbidity and mortality post-transplant for patients with leukemias, lymphomas, and myelodysplastic syndromes.

## Workaround

Standard HSCT uses unselected donor cells with aggressive immunosuppression to limit GvHD, a blunt approach that suppresses the beneficial graft-versus-leukemia effect alongside the harmful GvHD response.

## Entry wedge

Targeting hematologic malignancies (AML, ALL, MDS) where HSCT is already standard of care, but outcomes are poor. Orca-T replaces the same procedure with a precision-sorted cell product that can be administered through existing transplant infrastructure, without requiring a completely new care pathway.

## Method

Orca Bio's proprietary manufacturing platform uses high-resolution fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) to precisely separate and re-combine three specific cell fractions from donor material: hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs), regulatory T-cells (Tregs), and conventional T-cells. The reconstituted product, Orca-T, is designed to retain graft-versus-leukemia activity while suppressing GvHD through the immunomodulatory function of the Tregs. Phase 3 Precision-T study showed significant improvement in chronic GvHD-free survival vs. standard HSCT.

## TAM logic

Approximately 22,000 allogeneic HSCTs are performed annually in the US alone; global volume is roughly 50,000/year. If Orca-T achieves approval at a price point consistent with other CAR-T/cell therapies ($400-600K), and captures even 30-40% of the allogeneic transplant market, the revenue opportunity approaches $4-8B annually. (Source: CIBMTR data; company materials)

## Validated by

BLA accepted by FDA under Priority Review (initial PDUFA April 6, 2026; extended to July 6, 2026 following submission of additional CMC data). Phase 3 Precision-T data published in Blood: significant improvement in chronic GvHD-free survival vs. conventional HSCT. $250M aggregate financing (Series E + Series F, Lightspeed Venture Partners lead, closed January 2026), with additional $100M credit facility from SVB. Total funding approximately $625M+. Data presented at ASH 2025 and Tandem Meetings 2026.

## Competes with

Conventional HSCT (standard of care), Bristol Myers Squibb / Celgene (lisocabtagene maraleucel, axicabtagene), Novartis (Kymriah, CAR-T), bluebird bio, Vor Biopharma, Tmunity Therapeutics. Broader competitive landscape includes other allogeneic and autologous cell therapy developers targeting AML/ALL.

## Complements

Bone marrow transplant centers and transplant physicians (existing infrastructure delivers Orca-T), payer systems familiar with HSCT reimbursement codes, donor registries (NMDP/Be The Match).

## Value chain position

**Downstream of:** Donor cell collection, HLA matching registries, transplant center referral networks
**Upstream of:** Post-transplant monitoring, GvHD management, maintenance therapy, MRD surveillance

## Gaps

FDA BLA review extension (CMC data request) introduces uncertainty around approval timing and commercial launch readiness. Manufacturing scalability, precision cell sorting is inherently complex and hard to run at commercial volumes. Reimbursement path for a new cell therapy category requires payer engagement even after approval. East Coast manufacturing capacity is being built but not yet proven at scale.

## Notes

CEO: Nathaniel Fernhoff (co-founder, appointed May 2025). HQ: Menlo Park, CA. Manufacturing in Sacramento, CA (expanding to East Coast). Orca-T is composed of three precisely sorted fractions: Tregs (~10x enriched vs conventional HSCT), HSCs, and conventional T-cells, the Treg fraction is the key mechanistic differentiator. If approved, Orca-T would be the first precision-sorted allogeneic cell therapy to reach the US market.
